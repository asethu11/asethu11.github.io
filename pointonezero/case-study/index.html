The challenge

Northwind's 14,000 financial advisors spent the first 25 minutes of every client meeting preparing for it — pulling holdings, rebalancing rules, market context, and personal notes from six disconnected systems. The internal proposal to fix this had been on the roadmap for fourteen months. Nothing had shipped.

The asks were not unreasonable. They were simply too many. A new advisor portal. A market-data integration. A research summarizer. A CRM consolidation. Each of these would have taken a quarter on its own. None of them, individually, would have solved the meeting-prep problem.

Reframing
25 → 4 min

Once we defined the problem as "shorten the 25-minute prep window to under five minutes," the solution stopped looking like four products and started looking like one.

The frame

We brought the POINT / ONE / ZERO model into the engagement on day one.

POINT. The sharper tip of the sphere — the meeting-prep window — became the only thing the engagement would touch.

ONE. A six-person pod: one partner, one strategist, one designer, two engineers, and one advisor from Northwind embedded full-time for the duration.

ZERO. The zero-based model meant we asked, at every stage, "what's the smallest thing that solves this?" — and answered it with reusable agents from the library, not bespoke builds.

The approach

We compressed Northwind's traditional six-stage research process into 17 hours of synthetic prototyping. The pod ran four loops in parallel:

  • User research. 12 advisor interviews, synthesized by an agent into the three meeting-prep tasks that mattered.
  • Data integration. A single API surface — assembled from existing internal endpoints — that fed every downstream agent.
  • Agent orchestration. Eight agents from the reusable library: a holdings summarizer, a news brief, a regulatory check, a rebalancing recommender, and four more.
  • Interface design. A single-screen briefing layout, designed in the system we use for every engagement.

By the end of week one, advisors were testing a working prototype against real client meetings the next morning.

Before & after

Before

Six systems, 25 minutes

Advisors switched between portfolio analytics, CRM notes, market data, internal research, regulatory filings, and the client's personal household summary — manually reconciling the answers into a printout.

After

One briefing, four minutes

A single briefing surface, generated on demand for any client meeting. Eight agents fetch and synthesize in the background; the advisor reads, adjusts, and walks in prepared.

The outcome

Six weeks from kickoff to production. Eight weeks after launch, every advisor in the firm was using it daily — adoption was not mandated; it happened because the prep window collapsed.

The internal cost figure that mattered most to the Northwind leadership was advisor retention: prep-time was the most-cited reason for advisors leaving the firm. In the two quarters since launch, voluntary attrition is down a third.

Two quarters in
−33%

Voluntary advisor attrition, comparing the two quarters post-launch to the trailing four. The single biggest secondary outcome — and the one nobody scoped for.

What's next

The briefing surface is the first of three planned compressions inside Northwind. The same pod has begun a second engagement: replacing the eight-hour quarterly portfolio review with a continuously-updated thirty-minute one. The pattern, again, is reframing — not building.
