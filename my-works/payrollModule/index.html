Vertex Hub Payroll: Structuring Fragmented Payroll Operations

I designed a payroll module for Vertex Hub that replaced email-and-spreadsheet coordination with structured workflows. The module introduces dual-path submission, exception-focused review, and formal approval workflows with embedded validation and audit trails.

Mar–Apr 2026  |  Product Manager + Product Designer  |  B2B SaaS Platform Extension

TL;DR

Problem
Payroll teams spent 9,000+ hours annually managing spreadsheets across five systems. Schools submitted data via email with 75% first-pass accuracy. No centralized visibility, audit trails, or structured validation.

What I did
Led discovery (stakeholder interviews, workflow mapping), designed submission and review flows, defined 150+ functional requirements, established technical architecture for Q2 delivery.

Result
Launched with targets: >80% school adoption, >50% email reduction, <24-hour review cycles. Early signals: 3-minute submissions vs previous 20–30 minutes. Misconfiguration errors eliminated by design.

Context

Vertex Hub handles school operations—enrollment, compliance, reporting. Payroll remained outside the platform, running through 17 manual steps involving email reminders, Excel templates, Box uploads, manual interpretation, and double data entry across five payroll systems (Paylocity, ADP, iSolved, Paycore).

The numbers:

  • 9,000+ annual hours spent on spreadsheet management (24% of EdTec team time, 53% of PA team time)
  • 75% first-pass accuracy from schools
  • Data arriving <24 hours before payroll deadlines
  • Zero tolerance for errors (compliance requirement)

The process had no shared visibility, no audit trails, and no structured validation. Schools had no way to track cycle status. Specialists preserved every interaction in case they needed it later for corrections or compliance reviews.


The first round of information that we get from them is about 75% either accurate or in entirety.

— Danielle Kabak, Payroll Operations Lead

Constraints

Entity Variations

EdTec ran Paylocity-centered workflows with standardized processes. Pennsylvania used multiple systems with school-maintained spreadsheets and account-manager QC. Couldn't design for average—had to accommodate both while establishing patterns for eventual convergence.

Trust Requirements

Zero tolerance for errors meant validation architecture, audit trails, and human oversight weren't optional. Any design that bypassed human judgment in ambiguous cases would fail.

Excel Power Users

Some schools relied on VLOOKUPs, external data joins, and embedded formulas. Others just submitted basic rows. A single interaction pattern would guarantee low adoption from one segment.

Scope Pressure

Every conversation surfaced adjacent needs: Asana integration, SharePoint migration, state-specific checklists, KPI dashboards. Used phased roadmapping to protect v1 scope while documenting clear Phase 2–4 paths.

No Direct Integration

Couldn't connect to payroll systems in v1. Manual export would continue. Had to create value through submission clarity and review efficiency while building architecture for future automation.

Design Process

My Approach

I looked at the existing tools teams were already using—Asana, Box, and Vertex Hub—and mapped the new design to those familiar patterns. The goal was to automate everything that didn't require human judgment (reminders, emails, data entry, copy-pasting) and let specialists focus on what actually mattered: decision-making and verification.

This creates a mental model of Submit → Review & Triage → Approve & Export that matches how people already think about payroll cycles, just systematized.

Discovery: Three Time Horizons

Conducted 3–5 internal interviews with payroll specialists across both entities and 3–5 school interviews ranging from simple submitters to Excel power users. Didn't show prototypes first—needed to understand actual workflows before proposing solutions.

The key insight: had to design for three time horizons simultaneously—current state (what's happening now across entity variations), near-term transition (PA already adopting EdTec's standardized forms), and target future (structured data model for eventual API integration). This temporal layering prevented digitizing behaviors already being phased out while keeping the design buildable in Q2.

Review Interface: From Full Roster to Exceptions

My initial concept showed a full roster comparison with change highlighting—mirroring the internal payroll spreadsheet where every employee appears every cycle. Testing revealed visual noise: 90%+ of rows were unchanged in most cycles. Specialists scanned hundreds of irrelevant rows looking for the handful that mattered. Dawn Carey pushed back:

I don't want to see a bunch of people on a list if they didn't have data.

— Dawn Carey, Payroll Operations

She was right. This led to tabbed exception views—separate tabs for All, New, Terminated, Modified, and Flagged—with specialists landing on what needs attention by default. Unchanged employees available on demand but not in the primary review lane.

Dual-Path Submission

Discovery revealed two distinct school behaviors. Excel power users relied on complex spreadsheets with VLOOKUPs, external data joins, and embedded formulas. Simple submitters just filled in basic rows. When I started testing pure web-based concepts, a critical adoption risk emerged:

Clients aren't going to want to enter things directly. They're going to want to have a form that they can download, complete, and then upload. We need those two options to be available.

— School stakeholder interview

Forcing schools to abandon Excel workflows overnight would guarantee low adoption. The solution was two coordinated entry points: structured forms for salary changes (type-driven fields that adapt to the change type selected), and file upload for hours and complex scenarios. Both paths feed into the same review and validation system.

Testing & Iteration

Tested with internal operations leaders, working payroll managers, engineering leads, plus school contacts representing different spreadsheet sophistication levels. Used HTML-based, low-visual-fidelity but behaviorally rich prototypes—focused on whether specialists could find exceptions, whether issues were visible in the right places, whether the cycle model matched their mental model.

During technical feasibility discussions, the team explored AI-assisted document interpretation for uploaded files. Austin de Rossi's assessment was pragmatic:

That is a cool concept, but I think as a v1, it would cause a lot more confusion than actually be a benefit.

— Austin de Rossi, Engineering

Trust requirements in payroll meant any AI errors would destroy confidence. Building fallback flows for AI failures would add complexity, and the Q2 timeline didn't allow for adequate testing. We deferred AI interpretation to v2, focusing v1 on structured intake with deterministic validation.

The Design

School Submission Flow

Schools access cycles via magic links—cycle-specific URLs with 14-day expiry scoped to exactly one cycle. No account creation, no learning curve. The landing page orients schools immediately: what cycle is this, when is it due, what's the current status, what do they need to do.

Two coordinated entry points, both feeding the same validation and review system:

  • Structured forms (salary changes) — type-driven forms where selecting change type reveals appropriate fields. New Hire shows onboarding fields. Termination shows exit fields. Inline validation catches errors at point of entry—missing SSN blocks save with explanation, large salary increases (>20%) create a warning flag requiring comment but don't block submission.
  • File upload (hours & complex cases) — drag-and-drop with real-time processing status (pending → processing → validated/failed). Preview screen shows extracted data with validation issues highlighted. Never blocks the cycle if parsing fails—schools can proceed with manual handling if needed.

Both paths converge at a review screen showing all changes with color-coded validation status before submission. Submit button enables only when blocking errors are resolved.

Internal Review Workspace

The workspace is structured around five tabs: All Employees (complete roster for audit cases), New (hires needing onboarding validation), Terminated (exits needing final pay calculations), Modified (employees with field-level changes), and Flagged (all validation warnings and errors). Specialists land on what needs attention—clean records are hidden unless explicitly requested.

Changes-requested loop: When specialists need clarification, they flag specific employees with comments and mark the cycle "Changes Requested." Schools re-enter via magic link and see only the flagged employees—not the full submission. Schools correct those specific items, add response comments, and resubmit. Full comment history is preserved for both parties while the working surface stays focused on what needs action.

Approval Workflow

After payroll processing completes, specialists prepare an approval package with three required documents: Salary Report, Hours Summary, and Financial Package. Upload these, add comments for school context, click "Send for Approval." The school-facing approval interface uses a three-tab structure separating concerns: Employees (per-row approval with flagging capability), Financial Summary (totals and budget impact), and Documents (the three required uploads with opened indicators). Schools must either approve or flag every employee—they can't skip rows. All three documents must be opened before final submission.

Driving Closure: How I Locked the Design

The Decision Framework

Three weeks from ambiguous stakeholder requests to locked requirements meant I needed clear decision criteria. Every design decision had to answer three questions: Does this create measurable value in v1? Does this preserve the path to automation? Does this solve the adoption barrier or create a new one? These three questions converted debates into decisions—having explicit criteria prevented endless revision cycles.

Mental Models for Constraint Navigation

Entity variation: "Design for the union, implement for the intersection." The interface accommodated both entities' needs while the underlying data model found common patterns. One product that flexed to handle variations without fracturing into entity-specific codebases. Trust vs automation: "Automate the tedious, gate the critical." Reminders, status updates, validation checks—fully automated. Large salary changes, unusual patterns, ambiguous data—flagged for human review with clear context. Excel transition: "Meet them where they are, create pull toward structured data." Upload path works today. Structured forms show what's possible. Schools migrate voluntarily as they see the forms are faster.

Success Metrics as Design Constraints

Rather than treating metrics as post-launch evaluation, I used them as design constraints during development. >80% school adoption within 90 days ruled out any design that added friction at the entry point—led directly to magic links and dual-path submission. >50% email reduction required eliminating specific email-based workflows—led to the changes-requested loop where schools see only flagged items. <24 hours submission to review demanded exception-focused interfaces—led to tabbed workspace where specialists land on what needs attention.

Stakeholder Alignment Sessions

Three critical alignment sessions locked requirements and resolved design conflicts in real time. Week 1: walked through exception-focused tabs vs full roster with operations leaders using a live HTML prototype—team consensus landed on exception-focused after seeing the volume of unchanged rows in real cycle data. Week 2: presented dual-path decision to cross-functional group, framed as adoption risk mitigation, not feature complexity. Week 3: requirements lock—went through every contested feature with the decision framework and produced 150+ locked requirements with documented Phase 2–4 backlog rationale.

Post-Redesign Validation

After locking the design direction, ran validation calls with the same school contacts from discovery. Schools immediately understood the magic link access model. Excel power users confirmed they'd use the upload path initially but expressed interest in trying structured forms "once we see how it works"—validating the dual-path strategy and the migration hypothesis. Simple submitters preferred structured forms and appreciated the dynamic field logic. The changes-requested loop resonated strongly—schools liked seeing only flagged items. What changed from validation: clearer status messaging on the landing page and more prominent document-opened indicators in the approval flow. Shipped with zero major design changes post-validation.

Technical Alignment as Forcing Function

Weekly sessions with engineering weren't just feasibility checks—they were design validation. When engineering showed that reliable AI document interpretation would require 6–8 weeks of testing and fallback flows, that timeline conflict immediately clarified the decision: defer to v2, focus v1 on deterministic validation. These sessions converted design concepts into buildable specifications while the Q2 deadline eliminated everything that wasn't essential.

Outcomes

Delivered development-ready PRD with 150+ functional requirements, aligned cross-functional stakeholders, established technical architecture for Q2 delivery.

Target Metrics

  • >80% school adoption within 90 days of launch
  • >50% email reduction in payroll-related communication
  • <24 hours average time from submission to review complete
  • >90% approvals completed through Hub vs external processes
  • >4.0/5.0 user satisfaction from both schools and internal teams

Early Signals

Schools completing submissions in under 3 minutes versus the previous 20–30 minute process. Misconfiguration errors eliminated by design. Schools requesting other administrative workflows move into Hub. Expanding from 147 schools toward 200–300 as rollout continues.

The whole value is a structured request that we can eventually use to send off API calls.

— Jose Castellanos

The module established a structured data foundation for future payroll system integrations and created a retirement path for the internal payroll spreadsheet once direct system connections exist. Proved Hub can handle high-stakes, compliance-sensitive workflows with embedded audit trails and version control.
