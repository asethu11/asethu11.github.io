Solving Adoption Crisis (80% bounce-rate) With Limited Engineering Bandwidth

CreateAI Platform at Arizona State University is a RAG+LLM system that lets educators build custom AI bots to handle tutoring and repetitive student questions, freeing faculty time to focus on improving learning outcomes. The platform is highly customizable - 20 settings available for builders to tailor bots to their specific use case.

The adoption story: 147 courses, strong product-market fit. But these were early adopters, mostly technical users. When leadership set a goal to scale to 300+ courses, they realized they needed to reach the broader faculty population. The path to growth was unclear.

9-12 min read | UX/Product Designer | September '25 - Present | AI Platform for Edu

TLDR

Problem: Engineers designed it, early technical adopters shaped it. Non-technical faculty faced a platform bloated with unnecessary complexity, leading to a plateau in adoption rates.

Solution: Analysis of 8,000 projects mapped to two core jobs: Q&A (40%) and communications (25%). Branching off the platform, we designed focused-products with intentional constraints, automations, agentic layers, guardrails, that reduced cognitive load for non-technical users while preserving the original platform's flexibility

Impact: Q&A Bot showing measurable adoption across faculty. Expanding from 147 to 200-300 courses. Misconfiguration support tickets on track to drop from 20% to under 3%.

The Problem: Adoption Plateau Due to Complexity

Platform adoption hit a ceiling at 147 courses. Engineers built it, technical adopters shaped it, complexity won. 20 settings, RAG tuning, temperature controls. Options that made sense for power-users. A nightmare for everyone else.

Google Analytics told the story. 80% of new users bounced in under 2 minutes. Meanwhile, power-users were logging 2-3 hours daily, loving it. The platform didn't have a quality problem. It had a complexity problem.


Support data proved it. 20% of tickets weren't about broken features, they were about misconfiguration. Faculty drowning in options they didn't need, making mistakes they shouldn't have to make. Cognitive overload, dressed up as a technical platform.


The Challenge: Why Simple Redesign Wouldn't Work

When I presented these findings, product leadership resisted:
"We can't make drastic changes without proof. Engineering is at capacity."

They had a point. The 20 settings, RAG tuning, temperature controls—power-users needed them. Simplify and you break the platform for your best customers. This wasn't a UX fix. It was an architectural problem. Non-technical faculty needed a new product, not an improved one.

How do we serve both audiences without compromise?

Solution: Focused Products Over Platform Redesign

Analysis of 8,000 projects revealed the pattern: 65% of usage clustered into two jobs—Information Retrieval (40%) and Communication & Content (25%). The rest was noise.

The strategic pivot: instead of simplifying 20 settings for everyone, build standalone products with zero exposed settings. Automation handles complexity; users see upload → configure → launch.

Three layers made this possible:

  • Agentic automation (retrieval settings, conversation starters)
  • Pre-configured templates (working bot in 3-5 minutes)
  • Focused interfaces (no RAG controls, no temperature sliders)

Results

Q&A Bot is rolling out to new departments across the university. Early signals show measurable adoption among faculty groups. We're tracking setup completion rates, time-to-first-bot (goal: under 5 minutes), and misconfiguration support tickets (baseline: 20%, target: under 3%).

The adoption trajectory validates the product architecture hypothesis: we're expanding from 147 courses to 200-300 courses in the next phase, a 40% expansion driven by focused products rather than incremental platform improvements. Misconfiguration-related support issues are declining as guardrails and pre-configured templates prevent the most common failure modes.

This wasn't just a UX win. The product strategy shifted from "one monolithic platform for everyone" to "focused products by user job." This is now core to the product roadmap going forward.

Research: Understanding Where Faculty Get Stuck

I ran 20-30 moderated demos with faculty, staff, and instructional designers across departments (English, Biology, History, Business). The approach: let them explore the product cold, then guide them through setup while documenting where they froze.

Key finding: users who received structured guidance navigated significantly better. This confirmed the problem was information architecture and confidence, not feature complexity.

Friction points that appeared consistently:

Entry point confusion — Users searched for "Syllabot" (the product they'd heard about) but landed on "CreateAI Builder" (the platform). They questioned if they were in the right place within the first 30 seconds.

[IMAGE: Side-by-side screenshots - LEFT: What users expected to see vs. RIGHT: What they actually saw (CreateAI Builder dashboard). Annotation: "Users unsure if they're in the right product"]

Terminology barrier — The platform's purpose wasn't explained upfront. It was a blank canvas. Non-technical users faced terminology that meant nothing to them: LLM parameters, RAG settings, temperature controls, Top-K retrieval. These are CS concepts. An English professor asking "what does any of this mean?" wasn't being difficult. She was being reasonable.

False failure cycle — Users tested bots without uploading their course materials first, got nonsensical outputs, and assumed the product was broken. In reality, they'd skipped a critical dependency. But there was nothing in the interface preventing this mistake or explaining why it mattered.

Designing for the right audience — I was designing for 35-60 year old faculty, not Gen Z users. These users were intelligent domain experts, but they approached software differently. They expected clarity upfront. They wanted to understand what they were about to do before committing to it. They needed confidence before exploring. When a 48-year-old English professor landed on a blank builder interface with dropdowns for "RAG settings" and "temperature," she didn't think "I'll figure this out." She thought "this isn't for me" and left.

Stakeholder Negotiation: Aligning on Metrics and Scope

Leadership required proof before allocating engineering resources. The reframe focused on activation economics rather than UX problems: 80% bounce rate represented wasted acquisition spend. Converting 20% of dropoffs would achieve growth targets without additional marketing investment.

Finalized OKRs:

  • Scale from 147 to 300 courses by fiscal year end
  • Reduce bounce rate from 80% to <60%
  • Decrease misconfiguration tickets from 20% to <3%
  • Achieve time-to-first-bot under 5 minutes

Co-design sessions with engineering, product, and LMS teams resulted in three implementation paths: Canvas Syllabus auto-ingestion (primary), manual file upload (fallback), and hybrid approach. Engineering constraints around syllabus detection reliability led to Canvas integration as default with clear sync/non-sync messaging for supplemental uploads.

Canvas LTI integration was the critical partnership. Embedding Syllabot directly in course navigation eliminated workflow friction. Required coordination across IT, LMS, and security teams. Without this, faculty would still need to context-switch to external platforms.

Scope constraints kept engineering lift minimal: no core platform modifications, reused existing RAG infrastructure, automation layer handled configuration complexity. Approval granted based on contained risk, measurable success criteria, and clear exit strategy if metrics underperformed.

Technical Requirements & Architecture

The implementation required coordinating three core systems: Canvas integration for seamless enrollment, multi-path knowledge ingestion for flexibility, and distinct user flows for students versus faculty. All built as a standalone product on top of existing infrastructure.

Canvas LTI Integration

Direct integration into Canvas course navigation eliminated context-switching. Students and faculty accessed Syllabot without leaving their existing workflow.

  • Syllabot accessible via course navigation menu
  • SSO authentication through Canvas
  • Auto-enrollment of students as bot viewers
  • Course context payload (user role, course ID, enrollment data)

Knowledge Base Ingestion

Three-path approach balanced automation with flexibility. Canvas Syllabus sync was primary, but manual upload served as fallback for edge cases.

  • Primary: Canvas Syllabus tab auto-sync
  • Fallback: Manual file upload (.pdf, .docx, .txt)
  • Hybrid: Canvas + supplemental local files
  • Clear sync status indicators and timestamps

User Flows

Two distinct paths depending on role. Students got immediate access to published bots; faculty controlled publishing, preview, and knowledge source management.

  • Student view: immediate bot access if published
  • Faculty view: bot preview, source management, publish controls
  • Auto-detection of Canvas Syllabus presence
  • Graceful handling when syllabus content unavailable

Scope Constraints

Kept engineering investment lean by reusing existing infrastructure. Syllabot operated as a standalone product, not a core platform modification.

  • No modifications to core CreateAI platform
  • Reuse existing RAG infrastructure
  • Automation layer handles configuration
  • Standalone product, separate from main builder

Data Analysis: Which Products to Build

The standalone product strategy wasn't a guess. It came from systematic analysis of actual user behavior.

I wrote a Python script to analyze every project created on the platform (8,000+ projects). The script extracted and categorized custom instructions using LLM analysis. The raw results showed 70 different product categories - too granular to be actionable. I consolidated these into broader job-to-be-done categories by grouping similar use cases together.

What emerged was unmistakable: the vast majority of projects clustered into just two categories. Information Retrieval (Q&A bots) accounted for 40% of all projects. Communications (email rewriting, letter drafting, course communication) represented 25%. Together, these two use cases dominated 65% of the platform.

The remaining projects scattered across writing assistance (20%, but low-viability due to ChatGPT/Claude competition), decision support, planning, and other use cases. Decision Support had the highest quality metrics but represented just 159 projects.

This data-driven approach meant I wasn't guessing. I was responding to what users were already trying to do with our platform.

Rollout & measurement

Phase 1: Guardrails. The first intervention was defensive. We added guardrails to prevent the most common failure mode: users testing bots without uploading knowledge files, getting bad outputs, and assuming the product was broken. We auto-tuned backend settings based on document type and blocked bot testing without uploaded knowledge. This single constraint prevented the false failure cycle before it started.

Conversation starters also shifted from generic prompts ("Ask me anything") to hyper-specific examples ("What are the course prerequisites?" "When is the exam?"). These examples worked because they were answerable from actual materials, giving users immediate confidence. These aren't flashy solutions. They're defensive design - making the common mistakes impossible before users encounter them.

Phase 2: Super Templates. The next phase introduced pre-configured workflows. Instead of starting blank, users selected a template that came 95% configured. Saving and loading states provided clear UI showing progress through setup. Super Templates with pre-configured custom instructions and conversation starters meant users could have a working bot in 3-5 minutes instead of 20+ minutes of configuration.

Phase 3: Standalone Platform. The final phase was the strategic pivot: build separate products for separate audiences instead of trying to be everything to everyone. Q&A Bot became a fully automated, focused product: upload materials, set up conversation starters, launch. No manual configuration. No advanced RAG settings visible. No overwhelming options. The system handled complexity behind the scenes.

This automation-first approach paired with strategic LMS partnerships enabled ASU-wide adoption. Faculty could access Q&A Bot directly from their learning management system, removing friction from the workflow entirely.

Measuring Adoption and Ongoing Iteration

Q&A Bot is rolling out to new departments across the university. We're tracking setup completion rates, time-to-first-bot (goal: under 5 minutes), and misconfiguration support tickets (baseline: 20%, target: under 3%).

Early signals show Q&A Bot seeing measurable adoption among faculty groups. We're expanding to 200-300 additional courses in the next phase. This validates the strategic pivot: focused products outperform monolithic platforms for non-technical audiences.

We're still measuring full impact. Post-launch data is coming in. But the direction is clear: the activation problem is being solved differently than leadership initially expected. The strategy shift from "fix the complex platform" to "build focused products" is working better than incremental improvements could ever have.
